Bantayog Festival — A Celebration of History and Community

Bantayog is a province-wide celebration that honors the historic heritage of Camarines Norte and highlights local traditions, music, and community pride. The name "Bantayog" means monument, inspired by the First Rizal Monument in Daet.

About Camarines Norte

Hilagang Camarines — a province of resilience, heroism, and breathtaking natural beauty.

The Northern Frontier

Camarines Norte is strategically positioned as the gateway to the Bicol Region. Bounded by the vast Pacific Ocean to the north and east, and neighboring Quezon province and Camarines Sur to the south and west, its geography dictates its stunning environment.

The province is characterized by cascading waterfalls, lush mountains, and paradise-like beaches — most notably the world-class Calaguas Islands. Its capital, Daet, serves as the bustling central hub for travelers exploring the province's coastal and inland wonders.

A Journey Through Time

Camarines Norte's history is a story of resilience, rebellion, and a fierce desire for independence.

1573–1829 · Spanish Period
Ambos Camarines Era
Governed as one unit with Camarines Sur as "Ambos Camarines." Towns of Daet, Paracale, and Indan (now Vinzons) were established. Paracale's gold mining and goldsmithing skills were already thriving even before the Spanish arrived.
April 1898 · Revolutionary Era
The Daet Revolt
Katipunan leader Ildefonso Moreno led an uprising against Spanish colonizers, briefly occupying Daet. Though crushed by Spanish reinforcements resulting in Moreno's martyrdom, it ignited the flame of rebellion throughout the entire Bicol region.
March 3, 1919 / April 15, 1920 · American Period
A Province is Born
Act 2809 was passed by the Philippine Legislature on March 3, 1919, authorizing partition. Camarines Norte was officially declared a fully independent province on April 15, 1920 under Executive Order No. 22.
December 18, 1941 · World War II
First Guerrilla Stand in the Philippines
Just days after the bombing of Pearl Harbor, Camarines Norte became the site of the FIRST guerrilla encounter in the Philippines. Led by Governor Wenceslao Q. Vinzons, resistance fighters engaged the Japanese Imperial Army in Laniton, Basud.
The Heart of the North
Language & Identity
Despite being a Bicolano province, Camarines Norte boasts a unique linguistic identity. Due to its proximity to Quezon province, a slight majority of the population speaks Tagalog, while eastern towns lean toward a distinct Bicolano variation mixing Tagalog and Bicolano influences.
  • Home to the Manide Tribe — an indigenous community in the highlands of Jose Panganiban
  • Unique blend of Tagalog and Bicolano languages
Traditional Livelihoods
  • Agriculture: Coconuts, root crops, and the famous incredibly sweet Queen Formosa pineapples
  • Fishing: Mercedes is a major fishing hub supplying fresh fish and shrimp to Manila, famous for high-quality dried fish
  • Gold & Jewelry Crafting: Paracale continues centuries-old traditions of crafting fine gold jewelry including the agrimon and alakdan necklaces
Historical Figures of Camarines Norte

The province is the birthplace of some of the Philippines' most brilliant minds and fiercest patriots.

Wenceslao Q. Vinzons
WWII Guerrilla Hero · Provincial Governor
Born in Indan (now Vinzons), he was a brilliant lawyer, orator, and student leader. At age 24, he became the youngest delegate to the 1934 Constitutional Convention. When the Japanese invaded in 1941, he organized the armed guerrilla resistance in Bicol. Captured in 1942, he refused to collaborate and was executed — a true national martyr.
José María Panganiban
Propaganda Movement Writer · Linguist
Born in Mambulao (now Jose Panganiban). An exceptional intellectual with a photographic memory, he joined the Propaganda Movement alongside José Rizal and Marcelo H. del Pilar. Writing as "Jomapa" for La Solidaridad, Rizal himself praised him as "an excellent companion of labor and a legitimate hope of his unfortunate country."
Vicente Lukban
Revolutionary General · Born in Labo (1860–1916)
Born in Labo on February 11, 1860. He joined the Philippine Revolution and became a trusted general under Emilio Aguinaldo. As military governor of Samar, he led the Filipino resistance against American forces using effective guerrilla warfare tactics. Later elected governor of Tayabas Province after his capture in 1902.

How to Get There

Fly via Naga City
3-4 Hours
Manila (MNL) Naga Airport (WNP) Daet

The fastest route. Take a 1-hour flight to Naga Airport in Camarines Sur, then catch a passenger van or bus directly to Daet (approx. 2 hours).

Direct Bus from Manila
7-9 Hours
Cubao / Pasay Daet Terminal

Multiple bus companies (Peñafrancia, Isarog, DLTB) operate daily trips. We recommend taking an overnight sleeper bus for maximum comfort.

Drive from Naga City
1.5-2 Hours
Naga City Daet

Vans and buses depart regularly from the Naga City Bus Terminal to Daet throughout the day.

Island Hopping Access
Variable

There is no direct passenger ferry from Manila. Sea travel is strictly for local island hopping (Calaguas, Siete Pecados, Parola Island). Coordinate with local boat operators at Mercedes Fish Port or Vinzons jump-off points.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Camarines Norte safe to visit?

Yes! Camarines Norte is generally safe for tourists. The local government actively promotes tourism and the communities are known for their warm, welcoming hospitality. As with any travel, practice standard safety precautions, keep valuables secure, and stay updated on local advisories.

What is the best beach in Camarines Norte?

Bagasbas Beach in Daet is the most famous — it's the Surfing Capital of Bicol. For pristine white sand, Calaguas Island's Mahabang Buhangin is considered world-class. For a unique experience, visit Parola Island's pink sand beach in Jose Panganiban.

How far is Camarines Norte from Manila?

Camarines Norte is approximately 400–450 km from Manila. By bus, the journey takes about 7–9 hours overnight. The fastest way is to fly to Naga Airport and then take a van/bus to Daet, totaling about 3–4 hours.

What is Camarines Norte famous for?

Camarines Norte is famous for: (1) Bagasbas Beach — Surfing Capital of Bicol, (2) Calaguas Island — one of the most beautiful beaches in the Philippines, (3) The First Rizal Monument in the Philippines (in Daet), (4) Paracale's gold mining heritage, (5) The Black Nazarene Shrine in Capalonga, and (6) The province-wide Bantayog Festival every April.

Do I need a guide to visit Calaguas Island?

While not strictly required, hiring a local guide or joining an organized tour is highly recommended for Calaguas Island. The island is accessible only by boat and is quite remote. Local tour operators in Vinzons or Daet can arrange everything — boat, food, accommodations, and itinerary.

What are the must-try foods in Camarines Norte?

Don't miss: Angko (glutinous rice cake from Vinzons), Pandecillos (pili nut pastry), Sinantol (coconut milk and santol fruit dish), Kinunot (shark or stingray in coconut milk), Tinumok (taro leaf wrap), Tinapa of Mercedes (smoked fish), and Pili Tart & Roll from Daet.

What language do people speak in Camarines Norte?

A unique mix of Tagalog and Bicolano. Due to its proximity to Quezon province, a slight majority speaks Tagalog. Eastern towns speak a distinct Bicolano variation. Filipino (Tagalog) and English are widely understood, so communication is generally easy for visitors.

When is the Bantayog Festival?

The Bantayog Festival is celebrated throughout the entire month of April, making it a month-long province-wide celebration. It was first held in April 2005 for the 85th founding anniversary of the province. Key events include the Grand Parade, Binibining Camarines Norte, Street Dancing, and Parangal sa Natatanging Camnorteño.
